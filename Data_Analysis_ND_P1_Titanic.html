There are 177 records that have no age values. Hence a histogram of age of passengers would not represent the data correctly. If these records are replaced with 0 values then we would introduce outliers in data. This missing age data can also be replaced by mean age values using the following piece of code

mean_age = np.round(np.mean(titanic_df['Age']),decimals=1)

titanic_df['Age'].fillna(mean_age, inplace= True)

the mean(age) value is extremely over-represented in the dataset to the point where it does not resemble the original dataset. This is a big issue that could affect future statistical testing and analyses and should be a limitation that is pointed out</font>

# function to create plots for a particular variable
def create_compare_plots(df1,df2,key,kind,xlabel,ylabel,title):
    ax=df1[key].value_counts().plot(kind=kind)
    ax.set_xlabel(xlabel)
    ax.set_ylabel(ylabel)
    ax.set_title(title)
    df2[key].value_counts().plot(ax=ax,kind=kind,color='r')

What factors made people more likely to survive?¶

By the graphs above 'Survived' is dependent variable, that varies with independent variables 'Age', 'Pclass', 'Sex'. There seem to be a definite correlation between survival probability and these independent variables. Yet the statistics do not prove that being less in age, higher in class and female gender caused survival.

There could be few more dependency like presence of relatives 'Parch' (parents), 'Sibsp' (siblings), travelling nanny, location of 'Cabin' that could have affected the likelyhood of survival.</font>

Shortly after Trevor was born, the Allison family travelled to England for business purposes, and it was in England that young Trevor was baptised.

After the sinking, baby Trevor returned home to Canada, where he would be raised by his aunt and uncle, George and Lillian Allison.

About 33% of people who embarked from Southampton survived, 55% from Cherbourg survived, 37% from Queenstown survived. The location of the passenger on ship might have played important role on boarding the rescue boats. And location of passenger could be proportional to class and port of embarkment. Location of embarkment could also affect the duration of time already on ship (being sea sick) and mental ability to make sane decisions to save lives

If there were a way to figure out which passengers were crew members, then it would have been interesting to see how these graphs change.

The analysis presumes that any passenger under age 14 is considered a child passenger. Yet considering the era titanic sailed, there could be nannies and mothers under age 14. Hence the age data is highly limited and this fact could have affected the analysis.
